Route Planning

Plan and Add a Route

User can easily plan and add a route on the map interface. When user presses on the "add" icon, the control panel will appear on the right. User is able to enter in distance, angle in absolute and relative, and the starting point of the route.

Route Planning

Flexible Route Editing

User can also tap on the planned route to triger the "Route Editing" feature. User is able to see route data. He is also able to click and drag to edit an existing route, or he can type in new distance and route angle on the control panel.

Feature Mapping

Map Features from the Image Viewer

With "Feature Mapping", user is able to see obstacles from the Image Viewer panel. The sizes of the obstacles from the Image Viewer are pre-set. Users just need to drag unmapped obstacles from the Image Viewer to map on the interface.

Circumnavigation

Circumnavigate Through Obstacles

Users can click on an obstacle to activate circumnavigation feature. The map interface will show users a safe zone from the obstacle. Users can input start point, end point, radius, and direction of the rover to circumnavagate and take photos of the obstacle.

Design the Next Generation Space Rovers

CubeRover is a project in conjunction with NASA, CMU, and Astrobotics - a Pittsburgh company that has won a NASA Phase II Small Business Innovation Research (SBIR) Award - to develop this 4.4-lb. (2 kilograms) rover platform capable of small-scale science and exploration on the moon and other planetary surfaces.

As part of the CubeRover team, we thrive to design small, medium, and large-sized CubeRovers to support government agencies, companies, and universities throughout the world. Each CubeRover will be customized for specific science and exploration missions such as resource prospecting, in-situ resource utilization, geophysical investigations, and much more.

Design for CubeRover

Carnegie Mellon will fly CubeRover on Astrobotic's first mission in 2021 with NASA on the same mission to do blast ejecta characterization. The goal of Cuberover is to pave way for a variety of public and private missions in the future. Background Research

In order to locate where the CubeRover lands on the moon and improves communication between the rover and the controller, we utilize both absolute localization to relative localization to locate the robers on the moon.

Absolute Localization

Cuberover compresses and crops image from lander to reduce file-size to 41KB (10s of transmission). It then measures a distance on the lander which is of a known size and extrapolate that to estimate width of FOV (field of view) at the lander. It will use the width of the FOV to estimate the rover’s distance from lander. Then it will combine distance with known or estimated rotation data to find absolute location

Relative Localization

Back on earth, feature recognition is performed on a series of two or more overlapping images sent back from each move the rover makes. From those features we use a Simultaneous Localization And Mapping algorithm to determine how much and in what direction or rotation the rover moved. Each move is added to the last to give a best estimate of our current location relative to the lander.
